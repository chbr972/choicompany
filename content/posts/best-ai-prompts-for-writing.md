Most AI writing advice talks about which tool to use. This is about the other half: the prompts.

The same AI model produces wildly different output depending on how you phrase your request. A vague prompt gets vague output — competent-sounding but bland, generic, and not actually useful. A specific, well-structured prompt gets something worth editing.

I've refined these templates through actual daily use across email, blog posts, marketing copy, and editing work. Some I discovered by accident. Some came from embarrassingly bad first attempts at prompting. All of them produce noticeably better results than generic equivalents.

Copy them, adapt them for your context, and improve them as you go.

## The Core Principle: Specificity Is the Whole Game

Before the templates, one thing to internalize: the more specific your prompt, the more useful the output. This isn't just a tip — it's the entire mechanism.

Compare these two prompts:

**Vague:** "Write a blog post about coffee."

**Specific:** "Write a 1,200-word blog post for specialty coffee enthusiasts who are comfortable with basic brewing but want to understand the science better. Topic: why water temperature matters more than most people realize. Open with a surprising fact about how much a 5-degree difference changes extraction. Use a conversational but knowledgeable tone. Include 3 specific temperature recommendations for different brew methods with the reasoning behind each. End with one actionable experiment readers can try at home."

The first prompt produces something you'll delete. The second produces a strong first draft you'll spend 20 minutes improving instead of two hours rewriting.

The model knows a lot. Your job is to tell it exactly what you need, who it's for, and what good looks like.

## Email Templates

### Template 1: Drafting a Difficult Reply

```
I need to reply to this email:

[paste the email you received]

My position is: [what you want to communicate — be specific about your actual stance]
Tone: [professional and direct / warm but firm / apologetic but clear]
Key points I need to include: [list them out]
What I want to avoid: [e.g., sounding defensive, over-promising on timeline, being vague about next steps]

Draft a reply of about [150-250] words.
```

**Why this works:** Difficult emails usually fail because the sender wasn't clear on their own position before writing. This prompt forces you to articulate your stance explicitly, which makes the AI output substantially better — and often clarifies what you actually think in the process.

---

### Template 2: Cold Outreach Email

```
Write a cold email for the following situation:
- I'm reaching out to: [describe recipient — their role, company type, what they care about]
- My specific ask is: [be precise about what you want]
- Why they should care / my credibility: [brief — one or two sentences max]
- Length: under 150 words
- Subject line: [specific and non-generic — not "Quick question" or "Following up" or "I wanted to connect"]
- Do not use flattery, compliments, or phrases like "I love your work" unless I specify actual concrete details
- Lead with something relevant to them, not to me
```

**Why this works:** Cold outreach fails most often because it's too long, too vague, or leads with the sender's interests rather than anything the recipient cares about. Building those constraints directly into the prompt prevents the most common failure modes.

---

### Template 3: Summarizing a Thread for a Decision-Maker

```
I need to summarize this email thread for [role — e.g., my CEO, a client who wasn't part of the conversation]:

[paste the thread]

Write a 4-6 sentence summary covering:
1. What the issue was
2. What was discussed and what, if anything, was decided
3. Current status
4. What action, if any, is needed from [the recipient]

Use plain language. No jargon. Don't assume the reader knows the background.
```

This is one I use constantly. Long email threads are a productivity drain, and most executives or clients don't have time to read through 12 exchanges. A clear 5-sentence brief saves everyone time.

---

### Template 4: Saying No Professionally

```
I need to decline [request/invitation/proposal] from [describe person/situation].

Situation: [what they asked for]
Reason I'm declining: [actual reason]
What I want to preserve: [the relationship / leave the door open for X / keep things friendly]
Tone: [brief and clear / warm but firm]

Write a response under 100 words. Don't over-explain. Don't use vague excuses.
```

AI is surprisingly good at polite-but-firm decline emails, which are genuinely hard to write without either sounding cold or leaving the door open in ways you didn't intend.

## Blog Post Templates

### Template 5: Full Blog Post Draft

```
Write a blog post with the following parameters:

Topic: [specific topic — not too broad]
Audience: [who they are, what they already know, what they're trying to accomplish]
Goal of the post: [inform / persuade / teach a specific skill / entertain]
Tone: [conversational / formal / opinionated / technical]
Length: approximately [word count]
Structure:
- Open with [a surprising fact / a counterintuitive claim / a concrete scenario the reader has experienced]
- Include at least [N] specific examples with real details, not generic placeholders
- End with [actionable next steps / a clear recommendation / an open question]

Do not open with "In today's world," "In the digital age," or similar generic phrases.
Do not use filler phrases like "It's important to note that" or "It's worth mentioning."
Write like a knowledgeable person who has actually done this — not like a content generator.
```

**Why it works:** The specific instruction to avoid AI filler language makes a meaningful difference — models will default to certain phrases when not explicitly told not to. Specifying the opening type forces away from the "here's what this post will cover" lead that signals AI-generated content.

---

### Template 6: Opening Paragraph for a Post You've Outlined

```
I've written an outline for a blog post. I need a strong opening paragraph (150-200 words) that:
- Opens with a specific scene, question, or surprising fact — not a generic statement
- Creates genuine curiosity without clickbait
- Establishes the tone as [casual and direct / authoritative / conversational]
- Leads naturally into the rest of the post

Here's my outline: [paste outline]

The reader is [describe them — their background, what brings them to this topic].
Write the opening — don't explain your approach, just write it.
```

Opening paragraphs are disproportionately hard to write and disproportionately important. This template separates the work of writing a good opening from writing the rest of the post.

---

### Template 7: Headline Options

```
Write 10 headline options for a blog post about [topic].

Mix the following formats:
- 2-3 that lead with the specific benefit to the reader
- 2 with a counterintuitive or surprising angle
- 2 formatted as "How to [X] Without [Y]"
- 2 formatted as questions
- 2 that use specific numbers (e.g., "7 Ways to..." or "The 3-Step...")

Target reader: [brief description]
Tone: [casual / professional / punchy]

After writing all 10, mark your top 3 with an asterisk and briefly explain why.
```

## Marketing Copy Templates

### Template 8: Product Description

```
Write a product description for [product name].

Key information:
- What it is: [one clear sentence]
- The main problem it solves: [specific, not generic]
- Who it's for: [target customer — be specific]
- Key features to include: [list them]
- Tone: [practical / aspirational / conversational]
- Length: [50-100 words for e-commerce listing / 150-200 words for landing page]

Avoid vague claims like "best-in-class," "revolutionary," "industry-leading," or "state-of-the-art."
Be specific about what makes it useful. If there are numbers, use them.
```

**Why it works:** Specificity requirements prevent the vague superlatives that make product copy useless. If you force the model to be concrete, it produces descriptions that actually communicate value.

---

### Template 9: Social Media Post Variations

```
Write 5 social media posts about [topic/announcement] for [platform — LinkedIn / Instagram / X/Twitter].

Requirements:
- Stay within platform character limits ([LinkedIn: ~1,300 chars / X: 280 chars / Instagram: ~2,200 chars])
- Each post should feel meaningfully different — not the same message worded five ways
- Post 1: Lead with a direct question to the reader
- Post 2: Lead with a counterintuitive or surprising statement
- Post 3: Lead with a specific result, number, or data point
- Post 4 and 5: Your choice of angle — make them distinct from each other
- Include a clear call to action at the end of each post
- [For LinkedIn: professional but conversational, no corporate buzzwords]
- [For X/Twitter: punchy, no padding, hook in the first line]
```

---

### Template 10: Case Study / Success Story

```
Write a brief case study (300-400 words) based on the following:

Client/Customer: [describe without identifying if confidential]
The situation before: [what problem they had, what wasn't working]
What they did / what we provided: [specific actions or solution]
The measurable outcome: [specific numbers if possible — percentage improvement, time saved, revenue gained]
Timeframe: [how long it took]
One quote or key insight: [provide or ask AI to generate a plausible one to review]

Tone: [factual and credible / enthusiastic but grounded]
Audience: [other potential customers in similar situations]

Lead with the result, not the background. Don't bury the outcome.
```

## Editing and Polishing Templates

### Template 11: Tighten a Draft

```
Edit this for concision. Remove filler words, tighten passive constructions, and cut any phrase that doesn't add meaning.

Keep the voice intact. Keep all core ideas. Don't add new information.
Target: approximately [X% shorter / remove about 200 words].

Draft:
[paste text]
```

This is consistently one of the highest-value prompts in the list. AI is good at cutting — it doesn't have the psychological attachment to sentences that makes human self-editing difficult.

---

### Template 12: Rewrite for a Different Audience

```
I've written this for [original audience]. Rewrite it for [new audience], adjusting:
- Vocabulary and assumed knowledge level
- Examples (replace with ones more relevant to the new audience)
- Tone if needed

Keep the structure and argument intact. Don't add information I haven't provided.

Original:
[paste text]
```

---

### Template 13: Stronger Conclusion

```
This draft needs a stronger conclusion. Here's what I have:

[paste current conclusion or describe what you've written]

The article covers: [2-3 sentence summary of the whole piece]
The main takeaway I want the reader to leave with: [one clear sentence]

Write a conclusion of about 100-150 words.
Don't open with "In conclusion" or "To summarize."
Don't just restate what was already said.
End with something that either gives the reader a concrete next action or reframes the whole topic in a way that stays with them.
```

## How to Build on These Templates

**Save the ones that work for your use cases.** A prompt library tailored to your specific writing situations — your tone, your audience, your industry — is more useful than any generic list. These templates are a starting point.

**Add examples to any template.** For almost any writing task, adding "here's an example of the kind of output I'm looking for:" followed by a piece of writing you like dramatically improves results. Models calibrate to style examples very effectively.

**Refine in conversation.** You rarely need to write the perfect prompt in a single pass. "This is good, but make the opening more direct" or "The third paragraph is off-topic, remove it" — iterating in the same conversation is faster than rewriting the prompt from scratch.

**Note when prompts fail and why.** If a prompt consistently produces output that misses the mark, the problem is usually either an unclear instruction or a missing constraint. Tracking what goes wrong is how you get better prompts over time.
