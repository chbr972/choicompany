Over 60% of American adults don't have a will. Most assume it's something you need only when you're old or wealthy. Both of those assumptions are wrong, and they lead to real harm for families every year.

If you die without a will, you die "intestate" — and the state decides what happens to your assets, your children, and your possessions, according to formulas that have nothing to do with what you actually wanted. The process is public record, often slow, and frequently expensive. Family members who weren't on speaking terms end up in the same room fighting over your stuff while your kids wait for a judge to decide who raises them.

That's the worst-case scenario. But even in ordinary situations, dying without basic estate planning documents creates unnecessary headaches and costs for the people you leave behind.

I put off dealing with this myself for two years with the usual excuses — not old enough, not rich enough, too busy. When I finally sat down and did it, it took a Saturday afternoon and about $400. The procrastination was embarrassing in retrospect. For most people, getting the basics done takes a few hours and costs between $100 and $1,500 depending on your situation. Here's exactly what you need and how to get it done.

## What Actually Happens If You Die Without a Will

Without a will, your estate enters probate — a court-supervised process for distributing your assets — governed by your state's intestacy laws. These laws follow rigid formulas that may have no resemblance to your actual wishes.

**Intestacy laws determine:**
- Who inherits your assets (typically spouse first, then children, then parents, then siblings — in a fixed legal order)
- Who becomes guardian of your minor children
- How property gets divided when multiple heirs exist

**Where it goes wrong in practice:**
- An unmarried partner you've lived with for 20 years inherits nothing — only legal spouses are recognized
- Assets may go to an estranged sibling you haven't spoken to in a decade instead of a close friend you treated like family
- An 18-year-old child inherits $200,000 outright with no structure or restrictions
- A family member you would never choose may be appointed guardian of your young children
- If you have no qualifying heirs, assets may "escheat" — meaning they revert to the state

A will doesn't necessarily avoid probate (in most states), but it ensures your assets go to whom you choose, on your terms, with the people you selected managing the process.

## The Five Documents You Actually Need

### 1. Last Will and Testament

Your will is the foundational document. It does several important things:

- Names beneficiaries for your assets
- Names an executor (also called a personal representative) to manage your estate
- Nominates a guardian for any minor children — this is the single most important reason parents of young children need a will
- Can create a testamentary trust to hold assets for children until a specified age
- Specifies funeral and burial wishes

**What a will does NOT cover:**
- Assets with named beneficiary designations (retirement accounts, life insurance, joint tenancy property) — these pass directly to the named beneficiary, regardless of what your will says
- Assets already held in a living trust

### 2. Durable Power of Attorney (Financial)

This document names someone to manage your financial affairs if you become incapacitated — signing documents, managing bank accounts, paying bills, handling investments.

The word "durable" is important. A regular power of attorney terminates the moment you become incapacitated. A durable power of attorney specifically remains in effect during incapacity — which is exactly when you need it most. Make sure yours says "durable."

Without this document, your family may need to petition a court to establish a conservatorship to manage your affairs. That process typically costs $5,000–$15,000 in attorney fees and court costs, takes months, and is entirely public.

### 3. Healthcare Power of Attorney (Healthcare Proxy)

This document names someone to make medical decisions on your behalf if you can't make them yourself. The person you designate (your healthcare agent) needs to know your values and wishes and be willing to advocate for them — sometimes against what a hospital or other family members prefer.

Without this document, medical providers may default to immediate family members who may not agree with each other, may not know your wishes, or may not make the same choices you would.

### 4. Living Will (Advance Healthcare Directive)

A living will specifies your wishes about end-of-life medical treatment: whether you want life-sustaining treatment (ventilator, feeding tube, CPR) if you are terminally ill or permanently unconscious with no reasonable chance of recovery.

This document relieves your family of one of the hardest decisions humans face. Without it, that decision falls to whoever your healthcare agent is — or to a family who may be in conflict about what you would have wanted.

Many states combine the healthcare power of attorney and living will into a single "advance directive" form, which simplifies things.

### 5. HIPAA Authorization

This authorizes specific people to receive your medical information from healthcare providers. Without it, even your spouse may legally be blocked from getting information about your condition. It's a short form and costs nothing — don't skip it.

## The Beneficiary Designation Problem Most People Overlook

Here's something that catches people off guard: many of your largest assets don't pass through your will at all. They pass by beneficiary designation — meaning whoever you named on the account inherits directly, regardless of anything your will says.

Assets that pass by beneficiary designation:
- **Retirement accounts** (401(k), IRA, 403(b), pension)
- **Life insurance policies**
- **Bank accounts** with a "payable on death" (POD) designation
- **Brokerage accounts** with a "transfer on death" (TOD) designation
- **Real estate** held in joint tenancy with right of survivorship

If your IRA still lists your ex-spouse from a divorce 12 years ago, your ex-spouse gets that money. Your will cannot override a beneficiary designation. This is one of the most common and expensive estate planning mistakes people make.

**What to do right now:** Log in to every retirement account, insurance policy, and bank or brokerage account. Check the named beneficiaries. Update them if they're outdated. Also add a contingent beneficiary (the person who inherits if your primary beneficiary dies before you do) — without one, the asset may fall into probate despite the designation.

Review beneficiary designations after every major life event: marriage, divorce, birth of a child, death of a beneficiary.

## Do You Need a Trust?

For most people with straightforward situations, a will plus proper beneficiary designations is sufficient. Trusts add complexity and cost that isn't always necessary.

That said, a revocable living trust makes sense in specific situations:

**Avoiding probate:** Assets held in a living trust pass directly to beneficiaries without going through probate — faster, cheaper, and completely private. For people with significant assets, property in multiple states, or a desire for privacy, this can be worth the extra cost. Probate in some states can take 1–2 years and cost 3–5% of the estate's value.

**Protecting assets for minor children:** If you have young children, a trust can hold their inheritance until they reach an age you specify — 25, 30, or whatever you choose. Most parents don't want an 18-year-old receiving a large lump sum with no oversight.

**Incapacity planning:** A funded living trust can be managed by a successor trustee during your incapacity without court involvement — broader and more flexible than a financial power of attorney alone.

**Special needs beneficiaries:** A special needs trust lets you leave assets to a disabled family member without disqualifying them from Medicaid or SSI, which have strict asset limits.

**Large estate tax planning:** For 2026, the federal estate tax exemption sits at approximately $13.6 million per individual. Below that threshold, federal estate taxes don't apply. About 12 states have their own estate taxes with lower exemptions — check your state.

## What It Costs and How to Get It Done

### Option 1: Online Services (Simple Situations)

For unmarried individuals or married couples with children, few assets, no business interests, and no complicated family dynamics — online estate planning services produce legally valid documents at low cost.

| Service | Starting Price | What You Get |
|---|---|---|
| Trust & Will | $199 (will); $499 (trust) | Will, POA, healthcare directive — state-specific |
| Nolo.com | $99–$149 | Will drafting tools with legal guidance |
| LegalZoom | $89–$179 | Will package; attorney review add-ons available |
| FreeWill.com | Free (for simple wills) | Basic will; better for charitable-giving focus |

These are best suited to: single adults, married couples with straightforward assets, people without complex tax situations or family dynamics. Don't use an online service if you have a blended family, own a business, or have assets approaching estate tax thresholds.

### Option 2: Estate Planning Attorney (Complex Situations)

Work directly with an estate planning attorney when:
- You have a blended family (children from multiple relationships)
- You have a family member with special needs
- You own a business or significant business interests
- You own real estate in multiple states
- Your estate is large (approaching state or federal tax thresholds)
- Your family dynamics involve estrangements or potential disputes
- You have significant charitable giving goals

**Typical attorney costs:**
- Basic will package (will, POAs, advance directive): $500–$1,500
- Revocable living trust package (trust, pour-over will, POAs, directive): $1,500–$3,500
- Complex estate plans: $3,500–$10,000+

This is not the place to find the cheapest option you can. Estate planning mistakes don't get discovered until after you're gone, at which point your family pays the price.

## Your Six-Step Action Plan

**Step 1: List your assets.** Include bank accounts, retirement accounts, brokerage accounts, real estate, life insurance policies, business interests, and valuable personal property. Note which have beneficiary designations.

**Step 2: Identify your key people.** Who would you name as:
- Executor of your will?
- Trustee (if using a trust)?
- Healthcare agent?
- Financial power of attorney?
- Guardian for your minor children?

Ask people before naming them. Being an executor or guardian is a significant responsibility — don't surprise someone with it.

**Step 3: Update beneficiary designations on all accounts.** This is the highest-leverage action most people can take immediately. Log in, check, update.

**Step 4: Create your documents.** Pick the online service or attorney route based on your situation.

**Step 5: Store documents safely and tell people where they are.** Don't put originals in a bank safe deposit box — your executor may not be able to access it after you die. Use a fireproof home safe or a lawyer's office. Tell your executor and healthcare agent where to find everything.

**Step 6: Review every 3–5 years or after major life events.** Marriage, divorce, birth of a child, death of a named beneficiary, moving to a new state, or a significant change in assets — any of these require updating your plan.

## Common Mistakes That Cause Problems

**Not naming a guardian for minor children.** For parents of young children, this is the most important reason to have a will. Don't leave it to a judge.

**Outdated beneficiary designations.** An ex-spouse still listed as IRA beneficiary after a divorce is one of the most common and expensive estate planning failures.

**Creating a trust but not funding it.** If you pay for a living trust but never transfer your assets into it, the trust is essentially useless. Every asset needs to be re-titled into the trust name or it will go through probate anyway.

**Documents stored in a safe deposit box.** Your executor needs those documents. An inaccessible safe deposit box creates delays and costs.

**Doing nothing.** This is by far the most common mistake. An imperfect plan is better than no plan.

## Bottom Line

Estate planning isn't about being wealthy or old. It's about making sure your family doesn't have to make devastating decisions without guidance, fight over what you would have wanted, or pay lawyers to sort out what could have been settled for $300 online.

At a minimum, every adult needs:
1. A will — especially if you have minor children
2. A durable financial power of attorney
3. A healthcare power of attorney
4. An advance directive (living will)
5. Updated beneficiary designations on all accounts

The whole package costs $100–$1,500 and takes a day of focused attention to complete. Not having it can cost your family tens of thousands of dollars and months of unnecessary pain.

Do it now. The need can arrive at any age, with no warning.
