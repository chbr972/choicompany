If you own a home and have built up equity, you're sitting on a financial asset you can borrow against. Home equity loans and home equity lines of credit (HELOCs) both let you do that — but they're structured completely differently, and picking the wrong one can cost you thousands in extra interest or leave you scrambling for flexibility you don't have.

I looked into both options thoroughly when researching refinancing alternatives in early 2026, and the differences matter a lot more than most people realize.

## What Is Home Equity?

Home equity is the gap between your home's current market value and your remaining mortgage balance.

**Concrete example:** Your home appraises at $420,000. You owe $270,000 on your mortgage. That's $150,000 in equity. Most lenders let you borrow against 80–85% of the appraised value, minus what you owe.

Math: $420,000 × 85% = $357,000. Subtract the $270,000 mortgage balance. You could borrow up to $87,000.

Both products use your home as collateral. Default on either one, and you face foreclosure — not just a hit to your credit score. Take that seriously before you sign anything.

## What Is a Home Equity Loan?

A home equity loan is a one-time lump-sum loan with a fixed interest rate and fixed monthly payments. You borrow the full amount upfront and repay it over 5 to 30 years, just like a regular mortgage payment — except it sits on top of your existing mortgage as a second lien.

**What makes it work:**
- Fixed rate — rate is locked at closing, never changes
- Fixed payment — same dollar amount every month for the life of the loan
- Repayment starts immediately after closing
- Closing process typically takes 2–6 weeks

**As of April 2026, home equity loan rates** typically range from 7.5% to 9.5% for borrowers with good credit (720+ FICO). Credit unions are frequently running 0.5–1% lower than big banks — worth a call before you commit.

**Ideal uses:** A $45,000 kitchen renovation where you've signed a contractor agreement. Debt consolidation where you're paying off $38,000 in credit card debt at 22% APR. A medical bill you need to cover in full.

## What Is a HELOC?

A HELOC works more like a credit card than a loan. The lender approves you for a maximum credit line — say, $75,000 — and you borrow what you need, when you need it, during a "draw period" that typically lasts 10 years. After that, you enter a repayment period (usually 20 years) where you can no longer draw funds and must pay back what you borrowed.

**How it works in practice:**
- Draw what you need (minimum amounts vary by lender, often $100–$500)
- Pay interest only on what you've actually drawn during the draw period
- Variable rate tied to the prime rate — it moves when the Fed moves
- Minimum payments during the draw period often cover interest only

**As of April 2026,** HELOC rates are averaging around 8.5–10.5% depending on lender and credit score — but these numbers shift with Federal Reserve decisions.

**Ideal uses:** A whole-home renovation you're doing in phases over 2–3 years. Business working capital. Paying college tuition semester by semester. Keeping an open line as emergency backup.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | Home Equity Loan | HELOC |
|---|---|---|
| How you receive funds | Lump sum at closing | Draw as needed |
| Interest rate type | Fixed | Variable (prime + margin) |
| Monthly payment | Same every month | Fluctuates with balance |
| Interest accrues on | Full loan amount, immediately | Only what you've drawn |
| Rate risk | None after closing | Significant if rates rise |
| Flexibility | Low | High |
| Best for | One-time defined expense | Ongoing or phased expenses |
| Typical closing costs | 2–5% of loan amount | Lower; often waived |
| Draw period | N/A (you get it all upfront) | Usually 10 years |
| Repayment period | 5–30 years | Usually 20 years |
| Early closure fees | Usually none | Often 1–3% if closed within 2–3 years |

## Fixed vs. Variable Rate: Why This Matters More Than You Think

This is the decision that will affect you most. Here's my take after running the numbers on both scenarios.

**Home equity loan — the predictability argument:** You lock in your rate today. If your rate is 8.2% on a $60,000 loan over 15 years, your payment is $572/month forever. If rates spike to 11%, you're sitting pretty. The downside: if rates drop significantly, you're stuck unless you pay closing costs to refinance.

**HELOC — the flexibility argument:** You only pay interest on what you use. If you're doing a renovation project in phases and only have $15,000 drawn in month one, you're only paying interest on $15,000 — not your $75,000 credit limit. That's real money saved. But when the Fed raised rates 5.25 percentage points over 2022–2023, HELOC holders saw their payments jump significantly. That can happen again.

**A middle-ground option:** Some lenders — including many credit unions and banks like U.S. Bank — allow you to convert portions of your HELOC balance to a fixed rate. So you might draw $30,000, lock that portion in at a fixed rate, and keep the rest of the line variable. Worth asking about.

## What Each Option Actually Costs

### Home Equity Loan Fees

- **Closing costs:** 2–5% of the loan amount (covers appraisal at $300–$600, title insurance, origination fee, recording fees)
- **Origination fee:** 0–3% — lenders vary significantly here
- **On a $60,000 loan:** Expect $1,200–$3,000 in upfront costs

Shop at least three lenders. I found differences of nearly 1% in rate just from getting competing quotes from a credit union versus a national bank.

### HELOC Fees

- **Annual fee:** $50–$100/year at most lenders (some waive it)
- **Closing costs:** Typically lower than home equity loans; many lenders waive them entirely
- **Early closure/termination fee:** 1–3% if you close within 2–3 years — this catches people off guard
- **Inactivity fee:** Some lenders charge $25–$50/year if you don't draw on the line

One thing I noticed: lenders often market HELOCs as "no closing costs" — but read the fine print on early termination. If you close within 2 years, they recoup those costs through a fee.

## Pros and Cons Summary

### Home Equity Loan

**Pros:**
- Predictable payment makes budgeting simple
- Protected from rising interest rates after closing
- Good for people on fixed incomes or tight budgets
- Easier to plan around a specific expense

**Cons:**
- Higher upfront costs than HELOCs
- You're paying interest on the full amount from day one
- No flexibility — can't draw more if your project goes over budget
- Rate lock cuts both ways (you lose if rates fall)

### HELOC

**Pros:**
- Only pay interest on what you use
- Borrow, repay, borrow again during draw period
- Usually lower upfront costs
- Great as a financial safety net — costs nothing if unused
- Benefits automatically if rates drop

**Cons:**
- Variable rate creates payment uncertainty
- Draw-period-to-repayment-period transition can cause payment shock
- Easy credit access can lead to overspending
- Some lenders can reduce or freeze your line if property values drop

## Tax Deductibility: The Rule Most People Get Wrong

The interest on both products *can* be tax-deductible — but only if you use the funds to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home that secures the loan. That's straight from IRS Publication 936.

Using a home equity loan to consolidate credit card debt? **Not deductible.**
Using a HELOC to fund a vacation or pay tuition? **Not deductible.**
Using either to build a garage addition or renovate a kitchen? **Likely deductible.**

The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act eliminated deductibility for home equity debt used for non-home-improvement purposes. A lot of people haven't gotten that memo. Talk to a CPA before assuming your interest is a write-off.

## When to Pick the Home Equity Loan

Choose this option when:

**The project has a fixed cost.** You've got contractor bids in hand. The number is $47,000. You don't want a credit line — you want exactly what you need.

**You're consolidating debt.** Paying off $40,000 in credit card debt at 22% APR with an 8.5% home equity loan saves you roughly $5,400/year in interest. The math is compelling and the fixed payoff date keeps you honest.

**Rate risk keeps you up at night.** If you're borrowing a large sum and the idea of variable payments makes you anxious, fixed is worth it even if you pay slightly more upfront.

**You're on a fixed income.** Retirees and anyone with a predictable, non-expandable budget should generally favor the fixed payment.

## When to Pick the HELOC

Choose this option when:

**The project happens in phases.** You're adding a bathroom, then a deck, then finishing the basement — spread over 3 years. Drawing money as you need it means you're not paying interest on $75,000 when you've only spent $20,000.

**You want a backup emergency fund.** An approved HELOC you don't draw on costs almost nothing (maybe $75/year in annual fees). But it's there if your furnace dies, your roof leaks, or you face an unexpected income gap. For homeowners, this is one of the most cost-effective forms of financial insurance available.

**You have variable income.** Freelancers, self-employed business owners, and commission-based earners can draw during slow months and pay down aggressively during good months. The flexibility matches the income pattern.

**You expect to pay it back quickly.** If you're confident you'll eliminate the balance within 2–3 years, a HELOC often comes out ahead on total interest paid — especially if you make principal payments during the draw period rather than interest-only minimums.

## The Risk Scenario People Underestimate

HELOC holders face what I'd call "repayment-period shock." Here's how it plays out:

For 10 years, your minimum payments cover interest only. You've borrowed $65,000 over that time and paid interest-only minimums. At year 11, the draw period ends. Now you must repay $65,000 over 20 years — and your minimum payment jumps from maybe $450/month (interest only at 9%) to $585/month (principal + interest). And if rates rose during your draw period, it could be even higher.

This catches a lot of borrowers unprepared. If you go the HELOC route, build in a plan to actually reduce the principal during the draw period — don't just ride the interest-only minimums.

## How to Get the Best Rate on Either Product

1. **Pull your credit score before shopping.** Anything above 740 gets you the best tiers. Between 700–739 you'll pay about 0.25–0.5% more. Below 680, your options narrow significantly.

2. **Shop at least three lenders.** Include at least one credit union. In my experience researching this, credit unions routinely beat big banks by 0.25–0.75% on home equity products.

3. **Consider your loan-to-value ratio.** Borrowing 75% of your home's value gets you better rates than borrowing 85%. If you can put less on the line, do it.

4. **Time it with Fed signals.** If the Federal Reserve is signaling rate cuts, a HELOC might get cheaper soon. If they're signaling hikes, locking in a fixed home equity loan rate makes more sense.

## Alternatives Worth Considering

**Cash-out refinance:** Replace your entire existing mortgage with a larger one and take the difference as cash. Makes sense if current mortgage rates are lower than your existing rate. As of April 2026, this math rarely works out favorably given where mortgage rates have been.

**Personal loan:** No collateral required, no foreclosure risk. Rates are higher (typically 10–20% for good credit) and loan amounts top out around $50,000 with most lenders. Good for smaller amounts where you don't want to put your home on the line.

**0% APR balance transfer card:** For debt consolidation of amounts under $20,000, this can beat both home equity options. No collateral risk. Chase Slate Edge and Wells Fargo Reflect are worth comparing — both offer 0% for 15–21 months, with transfer fees of 3–5%.

## Bottom Line

The choice comes down to two things: do you know exactly what you need, and how much payment certainty do you need?

**Home equity loan:** You know the number, you want a fixed payment, you're covering a specific one-time expense. Done.

**HELOC:** You need flexibility, your expenses will come in phases, or you want a financial safety net with minimal carrying cost.

In both cases — only borrow what you actually need, have a concrete payoff plan before you sign, and don't forget that your home is the collateral. These are powerful financial tools used wisely. Used carelessly, they've put people out of their homes.
