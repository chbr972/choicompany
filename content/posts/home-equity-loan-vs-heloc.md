If you own a home and have built up equity, you're sitting on a financial asset that can be borrowed against. Home equity loans and home equity lines of credit (HELOCs) are two ways to access that equity — but they're structured very differently and suited for different purposes.

Choosing the wrong one can cost you thousands in interest or leave you without the flexibility you need. Here's a complete breakdown.

## What Is Home Equity?

Home equity is the difference between your home's current market value and what you still owe on your mortgage.

**Example:** If your home is worth $400,000 and you owe $250,000, you have $150,000 in equity. Most lenders allow you to borrow against 80–85% of your home's appraised value, minus what you owe.

Using the example above: $400,000 × 85% = $340,000. Subtract your $250,000 mortgage: you could borrow up to $90,000.

Both home equity loans and HELOCs use your home as collateral, which means failure to repay puts your home at risk of foreclosure. This is not debt to take lightly.

## What Is a Home Equity Loan?

A home equity loan is a lump-sum loan with a fixed interest rate and fixed monthly payments over a set repayment term — typically 5 to 30 years.

**Key characteristics:**
- You receive the full loan amount upfront
- Fixed interest rate (doesn't change over the loan's life)
- Fixed monthly payment (same every month)
- Repayment begins immediately
- Separate from your existing mortgage (a "second mortgage")

**Best for:** Large, one-time expenses where you know the exact amount needed — a major home renovation, a medical bill, debt consolidation.

## What Is a HELOC?

A HELOC is a revolving line of credit secured by your home. It works more like a credit card than a loan.

**Key characteristics:**
- You're approved for a maximum credit limit but only borrow what you need
- Variable interest rate (typically tied to the prime rate)
- Draw period: usually 10 years during which you can borrow and repay repeatedly
- Repayment period: typically 20 years after the draw period ends
- Minimum payments during the draw period often cover only interest

**Best for:** Ongoing or unpredictable expenses — home improvements done in phases, business investments, an emergency reserve, or college tuition paid semester by semester.

## Home Equity Loan vs HELOC: Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | Home Equity Loan | HELOC |
|---|---|---|
| Disbursement | Lump sum | Draw as needed |
| Interest rate | Fixed | Variable |
| Monthly payment | Fixed | Varies with balance |
| Best for | One-time large expense | Ongoing/variable expenses |
| Interest starts accruing | Immediately on full amount | Only on amount drawn |
| Risk of rate increase | None | Yes (variable rate) |
| Flexibility | Low | High |

## Interest Rates: Fixed vs Variable

This is one of the most important differences to understand.

**Home equity loan:** You lock in a rate at closing. If rates rise, yours stays the same. If rates fall, you're stuck with your higher rate unless you refinance.

**HELOC:** Your rate fluctuates with the market. Most HELOCs have a rate tied to the Prime Rate plus a margin. In a rising rate environment, your HELOC payments can increase significantly. In a falling rate environment, you benefit automatically.

In 2025, with interest rates elevated from the post-pandemic cycle, a fixed-rate home equity loan offers more predictability. But if rates fall, a HELOC will automatically reprice lower.

**Tip:** Some lenders allow you to convert portions of your HELOC balance to a fixed rate. This hybrid approach gives you some protection against rising rates.

## The Cost of Each Option

### Home Equity Loan Costs

- **Closing costs:** Typically 2–5% of the loan amount (appraisal, origination fee, title insurance)
- **Origination fee:** 0–3% depending on lender
- **No prepayment penalty** on most loans

On a $50,000 home equity loan, expect to pay $1,000–$2,500 in closing costs.

### HELOC Costs

- **Annual fee:** $50–$75 per year at many lenders
- **Closing costs:** Often lower than home equity loans; some lenders waive them (though they may recoup costs if you close the HELOC within 3 years)
- **Inactivity fee:** Some lenders charge if you have the line but don't use it
- **Early closure fee:** Common if you close within 2–3 years of opening

Shop lenders carefully. Credit unions often offer lower fees and competitive rates on both products.

## Tax Deductibility

The interest on home equity loans and HELOCs may be tax-deductible — but only if the funds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home that secures the loan.

Using a home equity loan for debt consolidation, a vacation, or paying tuition? The interest is generally **not** deductible.

Using a HELOC to fund a major kitchen renovation? The interest is likely deductible.

Consult a tax professional before assuming deductibility. The 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act significantly changed the rules, and personal situations vary.

## When to Choose a Home Equity Loan

Choose a home equity loan when:

**You know exactly how much you need.** If you're paying a contractor $45,000 for a home addition, you need that exact amount — not a revolving line.

**You want payment certainty.** Fixed payments make budgeting easier, especially on a fixed income or a tight monthly budget.

**You're concerned about rate risk.** If you're borrowing a large amount and don't want exposure to rising interest rates, a fixed-rate loan protects you.

**You want the money immediately.** Lump-sum loans are straightforward — take the money, start repaying.

**Ideal use cases:**
- Major home renovation with a fixed contractor bid
- Debt consolidation (especially high-interest credit cards)
- Medical expenses or other large one-time bills
- Funding an investment (e.g., down payment on rental property)

## When to Choose a HELOC

Choose a HELOC when:

**Your expenses are spread out over time.** If you're renovating room by room over 3 years, drawing from a HELOC as needed means you only pay interest on what you've actually used.

**You want a financial safety net.** An open HELOC you're not actively drawing from functions as an emergency line of credit, available if you need it. You pay nothing until you use it.

**You have variable income.** Self-employed individuals and freelancers with uneven cash flow can draw during slow periods and repay during strong ones.

**You expect to repay quickly.** If you're confident you'll pay back what you borrow within the draw period, you can take advantage of lower initial payments (interest-only minimum during draw period).

**Ideal use cases:**
- Multi-phase home renovation
- Business working capital
- Ongoing tuition payments
- Bridge financing

## The Risks to Take Seriously

Both products use your home as collateral. If you default, you can lose your home. This is categorically different from defaulting on a credit card.

**HELOC-specific risks:**
- **Rate increases** can significantly raise your monthly payment. Model out scenarios at 2–3% higher than your current rate before committing.
- **End-of-draw-period shock:** When the draw period ends, your minimum payment may jump substantially as you enter full principal + interest repayment.
- **Overspending temptation:** Easy access to a large credit line can encourage spending that accumulates into unmanageable debt.

**Home equity loan risks:**
- **Overborrowing:** Getting the lump sum all at once can encourage taking more than you need.
- **Rate lock regret:** If rates drop significantly, you'll need to refinance to capture the lower rate (and pay closing costs again).

## How to Get the Best Rate

For either product:

1. **Shop multiple lenders.** Rates vary significantly between banks, credit unions, and online lenders. Get at least 3 quotes.
2. **Improve your credit score first.** Rates on home equity products are heavily influenced by your FICO score. A 760+ score will get you materially better terms than a 680.
3. **Maintain a low loan-to-value ratio.** Lenders reward borrowers who aren't maxing out their equity.
4. **Consider credit unions.** They often offer lower fees and competitive rates compared to large commercial banks.

## Alternatives to Consider

If you're not sure either product is right for you:

**Cash-out refinance:** Replace your entire mortgage with a new, larger mortgage and take the difference in cash. This may offer lower rates but resets your mortgage term and has higher closing costs.

**Personal loan:** No home collateral required, but rates are higher and amounts are typically limited.

**0% balance transfer card:** For debt consolidation of smaller amounts, this can be cheaper than any secured product.

## Bottom Line

If you need a specific, large sum for a defined purpose and want payment predictability: **home equity loan**.

If you want flexibility, will draw funds over time, or want a financial safety net: **HELOC**.

In both cases, borrow only what you need, have a clear repayment plan, and remember that your home is on the line. Used responsibly, home equity products offer some of the most affordable borrowing available to homeowners. Used carelessly, they can cost you the home itself.
