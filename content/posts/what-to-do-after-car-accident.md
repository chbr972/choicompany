A car accident is one of the situations where decisions you make in the first 10 minutes can cost you thousands of dollars — or protect you from losing them. Most people are unprepared. They're in shock, their car is damaged, the other driver may be hostile or trying to talk them out of calling the police, and they're making consequential choices with no framework for what matters.

I've spoken with personal injury attorneys about the most common mistakes people make after accidents, and the patterns repeat constantly. People apologize at the scene. They skip the ER because they feel fine. They give recorded statements to the other driver's insurance company within 24 hours. Each of these costs them later.

Here is a clear, step-by-step plan you can follow if this happens to you.

## In the First Minutes: At the Scene

### Step 1: Stop. Don't leave the scene.

Leaving the scene of an accident is a crime in every state, even if the accident was minor. The legal term is "hit and run," and penalties range from fines to felony charges depending on whether anyone was injured.

Stop your vehicle safely. Turn on your hazard lights. Check yourself and your passengers for injuries.

### Step 2: Call 911 if Anyone Is Injured

If anyone is hurt — including yourself — call 911 immediately. Don't attempt to move injured people unless they're in immediate danger from fire or oncoming traffic. Moving someone with a spinal injury can cause permanent paralysis.

### Step 3: Move to Safety if You Can

If the accident is minor, vehicles are drivable, and no one is seriously injured, move them to the shoulder or a parking lot to clear the roadway. This is the law in most states — staying in the lane of travel after a minor fender-bender creates additional accident risk and may itself be illegal.

If vehicles can't move, get yourself and passengers away from traffic. Stay behind a barrier if possible.

### Step 4: Call the Police — Even for Minor Accidents

Legally, you're required to report accidents in most states when:
- Anyone is injured
- Property damage exceeds a threshold (typically $500–$1,000, but it varies by state)

But even when it's not legally required, call for a police report when:
- The other driver is uncooperative, hostile, or seems impaired
- There's any dispute about what happened
- The damage looks significant
- You have passengers who may claim injuries later
- The other driver wants to "handle it without insurance"

**Why the police report matters:** An official police report documenting the facts is far more valuable in an insurance claim dispute than competing memories from two drivers. Without it, any liability dispute becomes your word versus theirs. Insurance adjusters take police reports seriously. They view the absence of one as a disadvantage for your claim.

### Step 5: Document Everything Before Anyone Leaves

Use your phone. Take more photos than you think you need.

**Photos to take:**
- Both vehicles from multiple angles — front, back, driver's side, passenger side
- Close-ups of all damage on both vehicles
- License plates of all involved vehicles
- The full accident scene, including street signs, traffic signals, skid marks, and road markings
- Weather and road conditions
- Any injuries visible on yourself (document promptly — bruising often worsens over the first 24–48 hours)
- Debris field or fluids on the road

**Information to collect:**
- Full name, address, and phone number of the other driver
- Driver's license number and state
- License plate number and state
- Vehicle make, model, year, and color
- Insurance company name and policy number (photograph the insurance card)
- Name, badge number, and department of responding police officer
- Names and contact information of any witnesses
- Exact location (intersection, mile marker, nearest cross street)

Do this systematically before you feel better and think you'll remember. You won't.

## What to Say — and What Not to Say

This is where people lose their claims before they even know there's a claim to protect.

### Say:
- Factual observations only: "The other vehicle ran the red light" if you saw it clearly
- Your identification information when asked by police
- Cooperate fully with the responding officer's investigation

### Do Not Say:
**"I'm sorry."** This is instinct. Don't do it. "I'm sorry" is an apology that can be — and has been — used as an admission of liability in insurance claims and litigation. You don't have all the facts in the chaotic first minutes after an accident. Liability is a legal determination made after investigation, not a reflex.

**"I'm fine."** You don't know that yet. Adrenaline masks pain. Whiplash, soft tissue injuries, and concussions frequently have delayed symptom onset — hours to days after the accident. Don't state you have no injuries at the scene.

**"It was partly my fault."** Even if you were distracted or contributed to the accident, liability in most states is a matter of percentage (comparative fault). What you say at the scene can be used against you.

**"I'll accept cash to settle this."** Cash settlements at the scene are almost always a bad deal for you. Vehicle damage may be worse than it looks. Injuries may not be apparent yet. Once you accept cash and agree not to involve insurance, you give up any future claims — even if your neck still hurts three weeks later and you need physical therapy.

**Let the other driver talk you out of calling police.** "The other driver said his insurance would handle it and we don't need to make this complicated." This is almost always said in the interest of the other driver. Police reports create official records that protect you.

## After You Leave the Scene

### Get Medical Attention Within 24–48 Hours

Even if you feel fine. This is important for two reasons.

First, many common accident injuries — whiplash, soft tissue tears, concussion, herniated discs — have delayed symptoms. The adrenaline of a crash suppresses pain signals. You may feel stiff and sore the next morning. By day three, you may barely be able to turn your neck. Getting checked out promptly means a physician is documenting your condition while it's contemporaneous with the accident.

Second, the gap in time between an accident and your first medical visit is a standard defense used by insurance companies. If you felt fine and waited two weeks to see a doctor, their adjuster will argue your injuries either didn't result from the accident or aren't as serious as claimed. Prompt medical attention creates a documented record that's hard to dispute.

Go to an ER, urgent care, or your primary care doctor. Tell them you were in an accident and describe all symptoms — even minor ones. Document everything.

### Report to Your Insurance Company Promptly

Most auto insurance policies require notification "as soon as reasonably practicable" after an accident. Failing to report promptly can — in some cases — give your insurer grounds to deny coverage.

When you report:
- Give factual information: date, time, location, other vehicle's information, police report number
- Don't speculate about liability or describe injuries you haven't been examined for
- Ask your insurer what steps to take and what coverage you have (collision, UM/UIM, MedPay)

**Regarding the other driver's insurer:** Their insurance company may call you for a recorded statement within 24–48 hours of the accident. You are not legally required to give one. Many personal injury attorneys advise strongly against giving recorded statements to an adverse insurer until you've spoken with your own insurance company or consulted an attorney, particularly if you have any injuries. For a minor fender-bender with no injuries and clear liability, cooperation is usually fine. For anything involving injuries or disputed fault, be cautious.

### Get the Police Report

Police reports typically become available within 5–14 days. Get a copy.

Review it carefully. Check that the names, plate numbers, insurance information, and the officer's narrative are accurate. If there are factual errors, you can usually file a supplemental report with the department to correct the record.

### Save Every Record Related to the Accident

From day one, keep:
- All medical bills and records (ER visit, imaging, physical therapy, specialists)
- Prescription receipts
- Documentation of missed work days and lost wages (pay stubs, employer letter)
- Transportation costs to medical appointments (mileage, Uber/Lyft receipts)
- Receipts for any accident-related expenses (rental car if collision is covered, etc.)
- Written record of how your injuries affect your daily life

This documentation is what converts a valid claim into a quantifiable settlement.

## Understanding Your Insurance Coverage

Depending on your policy and your state's laws, several coverages may apply after an accident:

**Liability:** Pays for damage and injuries you caused to other people. Your insurer defends you and pays claims up to your policy limits.

**Collision:** Pays to repair your vehicle regardless of fault — minus your deductible. If the accident was your fault or the other driver is uninsured, this is typically how your car gets repaired.

**Uninsured/Underinsured Motorist (UM/UIM):** Pays your medical expenses and sometimes vehicle damage when the at-fault driver has no insurance or not enough insurance. Approximately 1 in 8 drivers in the U.S. is uninsured. This coverage is critical.

**Medical Payments (MedPay) / Personal Injury Protection (PIP):** Pays your medical bills regardless of who caused the accident. Useful to cover out-of-pocket costs immediately, before a liability determination is made. PIP is mandatory in no-fault states.

**No-fault insurance states:** Florida, Michigan, New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and several others require you to use your own PIP coverage for injuries first, regardless of who caused the accident — before pursuing the at-fault driver's liability coverage.

## When to Contact a Personal Injury Attorney

Contact an attorney when:
- You have any injuries beyond minor (anything beyond scrapes and a brief headache)
- The other driver is disputing fault
- The insurance company's initial offer doesn't cover your documented losses
- You missed work and are claiming lost wages
- You have injuries that may affect you long-term
- A commercial vehicle (delivery truck, rideshare, semi) was involved
- A government vehicle was involved (different filing rules and shorter deadlines apply)
- Multiple vehicles or parties are involved
- Your claim is being denied

**Initial consultations are free at almost every personal injury firm.** Personal injury attorneys work on contingency — they take a percentage of your recovery (typically 33% pre-litigation, 40% if it goes to trial), which means you owe nothing unless you win.

When I researched this topic, attorneys I spoke with consistently said the same thing: the biggest mistake people make is settling too quickly, before they understand the full scope of their injuries.

**Don't wait too long:** Statutes of limitations apply to personal injury claims, and they vary by state:
- California, Texas, Florida: 2 years from the date of the accident
- New York, Massachusetts: 3 years
- Some states: 1 year for injury claims, shorter for government entities

Waiting until year two to consult an attorney about a serious injury is cutting it very close. Consult earlier.

## Common Mistakes That Undermine Claims

**Posting about the accident on social media.** Insurance investigators and opposing attorneys search social media. A photo of you hiking two weeks after claiming a serious back injury — even from a different context, even before the accident — can be used to question your credibility. Keep the accident off social media entirely.

**Missing medical appointments.** When there are gaps in your treatment, insurers and attorneys for the other side argue those gaps mean you weren't seriously injured or your condition resolved. Attend every scheduled appointment. If you can't attend, reschedule and document why.

**Accepting the first settlement offer.** Insurance companies routinely make early offers before the full extent of injuries is known. Once you sign a release and accept a settlement, you cannot reopen the claim — even if you discover months later that your herniated disc requires surgery. Do not settle until you (and ideally an attorney) understand the full picture of your injuries and their long-term implications.

**Not documenting lost wages.** If injuries caused you to miss work, this is compensable. Get a letter from your employer on company letterhead documenting the dates missed and your daily or hourly wage. Without documentation, this claim is much harder to support.

**Assuming you can't recover anything because you were partially at fault.** Most states use comparative fault rules — you can still recover compensation even if you were 30% at fault for the accident, though your recovery is reduced by your percentage of fault. A few states use contributory negligence (if you were any percent at fault, you may not be able to recover), but these are a minority. Consult an attorney before assuming fault eliminates your claim.

## What a Fair Settlement Covers

In a personal injury claim, you may be entitled to:

**Economic damages (specific, calculable losses):**
- Medical expenses — past bills and projected future treatment costs
- Lost wages — actual missed work plus reduced earning capacity if injuries affect long-term employment
- Property damage — repair or replacement value of your vehicle and any other damaged property
- Out-of-pocket expenses — co-pays, prescription costs, rental car costs, transportation to appointments

**Non-economic damages (subjective losses):**
- Pain and suffering — compensation for physical pain experienced and expected
- Emotional distress — anxiety, PTSD, and psychological impact of the accident
- Loss of enjoyment of life — activities you can no longer participate in due to injuries
- Loss of consortium — impact on your relationship with your spouse or family

Non-economic damages are often where significant money is at stake in serious injury cases. They're also where you most benefit from an experienced attorney who understands what juries in your jurisdiction typically award.

## Save This for Later

Car accidents are rare enough that most people are unprepared when they happen. Save this guide to your phone — screenshots work fine — or bookmark it. In the chaos of an actual accident, having a checklist is genuinely valuable.

The core principles:
1. Safety first — check for injuries, call 911
2. Document everything before leaving the scene
3. Don't admit fault, apologize, or accept cash
4. Get medical attention within 24–48 hours even if you feel fine
5. Report promptly to your insurer
6. Don't give recorded statements to the other driver's insurer without guidance
7. Don't settle quickly when injuries are involved
8. Consult a personal injury attorney for anything beyond a minor fender-bender

These steps protect you. Following them won't guarantee a perfect outcome, but skipping them will cost you.
