We've all read the listicles. Wake up at 5 AM. Take cold showers. Meditate for an hour. Some of it helps — but most productivity advice ignores how you actually work.

Here are ten habits that hold up under real scrutiny.

## 1. Time-Block Your Calendar, Not Just Your Tasks

A to-do list tells you *what* to do. A time-blocked calendar tells you *when*. Schedule your highest-leverage work in blocks during your peak cognitive hours — usually the first two to four hours after you start your day.

Don't schedule every minute. Leave buffers. Life happens.

## 2. Kill Notifications at the Source

The average knowledge worker checks their phone 96 times per day. Every interruption costs not just the moment itself but up to 23 minutes to fully regain focus.

Turn off push notifications for email, Slack, and social media by default. Batch-check them at scheduled times — twice a day is usually enough.

## 3. End Each Day With a Three-Item Tomorrow List

Before you close your laptop, write down the three things that *must* get done tomorrow. Just three. This means your next morning starts with intent rather than inbox-driven chaos.

## 4. Use the Two-Minute Rule Ruthlessly

If a task takes fewer than two minutes, do it immediately. The overhead of capturing, prioritizing, and revisiting a small task often costs more than the task itself.

## 5. Protect Your Mornings

Your willpower and focus are finite resources that deplete throughout the day. Spend the first 90 minutes on deep work — creative thinking, complex writing, difficult problem-solving. Save email and meetings for the afternoon.

## 6. Single-Task with Intention

Multitasking is a myth. What we call multitasking is actually task-switching, and it makes both tasks worse. Close tabs. Put on noise-canceling headphones. Work on one thing until it's done or you've hit your time block.

## 7. Track Time for One Week Per Quarter

Most people have no idea where their time actually goes. Use a time-tracking tool like Toggl for one week every three months. The results are usually shocking and immediately actionable.

## 8. Say No Faster

Every yes is a no to something else — usually to your own priorities. Practice the art of the polite, fast no. "That's not something I can take on right now" is a complete sentence.

## 9. Review Weekly, Reset Monthly

A weekly review (15–30 minutes) helps you catch falling balls and realign with goals. A monthly reset helps you zoom out, evaluate what's working, and decide what to stop doing.

## 10. Make Rest a Priority, Not a Reward

Sleep deprivation destroys cognitive performance more than almost anything else. Eight hours isn't lazy — it's a performance edge. Similarly, real vacations, real weekends, and genuine downtime make the work hours more effective.

---

Productivity isn't about doing more. It's about doing the right things well. Start with two or three of these habits, embed them, and then layer in more.
