Your home is likely your most valuable asset. Homeowners insurance protects that asset — but the details of what's actually covered can surprise you, especially when you file a claim and discover a gap you didn't know existed.

This guide explains what standard homeowners insurance covers, where the most common gaps are, and how to make sure you have the right protection.

## What Standard Homeowners Insurance Covers

A standard homeowners insurance policy (HO-3, the most common type) provides several categories of coverage:

### Dwelling Coverage (Coverage A)

Pays to repair or rebuild your home's structure if it's damaged by a covered peril. This includes the walls, roof, foundation, built-in appliances, and attached structures like a garage.

**Covered perils typically include:**
- Fire and smoke
- Windstorm and hail
- Lightning
- Vandalism and malicious mischief
- Theft
- Weight of snow and ice
- Explosion
- Water damage from internal sources (burst pipes, not flooding)

**How much you need:** Your dwelling coverage should equal the cost to **rebuild** your home — not its market value. In areas with high construction costs, rebuilding costs can exceed market value. Undercovering your dwelling is one of the most expensive mistakes homeowners make.

### Other Structures (Coverage B)

Covers detached structures on your property — fences, detached garages, sheds, pool houses. Typically set at 10% of your dwelling coverage.

### Personal Property (Coverage C)

Covers your belongings — furniture, electronics, clothing, appliances — if they're damaged or stolen, whether at home or elsewhere.

**Standard amount:** 50–70% of dwelling coverage, though you should calculate your actual possessions' value.

**Replacement cost vs. actual cash value:** This distinction matters enormously.
- **Actual cash value (ACV):** Pays what your item is worth today after depreciation. A 5-year-old laptop that cost $1,500 new might be valued at $400.
- **Replacement cost value (RCV):** Pays what it costs to replace the item with a comparable new one. That same laptop gets you $1,500.

Always choose replacement cost coverage if your policy allows it. The premium difference is modest; the claim difference can be enormous.

### Loss of Use / Additional Living Expenses (Coverage D)

If your home is uninhabitable due to a covered loss, this pays for hotel stays, meals, and other costs while repairs are made. Typically 20–30% of dwelling coverage.

### Personal Liability (Coverage E)

Pays if someone is injured on your property and sues you, or if you accidentally damage someone else's property. Covers legal defense and judgments.

Standard limits: $100,000–$300,000. **This is often too low** — consider umbrella insurance for additional liability coverage.

### Medical Payments (Coverage F)

Pays medical bills for guests injured on your property regardless of fault. Typically $1,000–$5,000. Designed for minor incidents.

## What Standard Homeowners Insurance Does NOT Cover

The gaps are where many homeowners get hurt:

### Flooding

**Standard homeowners insurance does not cover flooding.** Not from rain, storm surge, overflowing rivers, or surface water. This shocks many homeowners who discover it after a flood.

Flood insurance is purchased separately through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or private flood insurers. Premiums vary widely based on location and flood zone.

If you live in a flood-prone area or a FEMA-designated high-risk flood zone, separate flood insurance is essential. But floods occur outside designated flood zones too — 20% of flood insurance claims come from moderate-to-low risk areas.

### Earthquakes

Earthquake damage is excluded from standard policies. Earthquake insurance must be purchased separately, either as a rider or a standalone policy. If you live in an earthquake-prone region (Pacific Coast, parts of the Midwest, New Zealand), evaluate whether you need this coverage.

### Sewer Backup

Water backing up from sewers or drains is typically excluded. This can cause catastrophic damage to a finished basement. Sewer backup coverage is often available as an affordable endorsement — usually $50–$100/year for meaningful coverage.

### Mold

Mold caused by a covered loss (e.g., a burst pipe) may be covered. Mold resulting from ongoing maintenance issues (persistent humidity, slow leaks you ignored) typically is not.

### Normal Wear and Tear

Insurance covers sudden, accidental losses — not gradual deterioration. A 20-year-old roof that fails isn't covered; a roof that was damaged by hail last week is.

### Home Business

Running a business from home introduces liability and property exposures that standard homeowners policies don't cover. If you have significant business inventory, equipment, or client visitors, you need separate business coverage or an endorsement.

### High-Value Items

Standard personal property coverage often has sublimits for certain categories:
- Cash: $200–$500
- Jewelry/watches: $1,500–$2,500
- Firearms: $2,000–$2,500
- Art, antiques, collectibles: Often limited
- Electronics: May have a sublimit

If your jewelry, art, or other valuables exceed these limits, schedule them as separate items ("scheduled personal property") or purchase a floater policy. These items are insured for their appraised value with no deductible in many cases.

## Common Coverage Mistakes

**Insuring for market value instead of rebuild cost.** Market value includes land — which can't be destroyed. Your insurance should cover the cost to rebuild your home's structure and is often different from what you'd sell the house for.

**Not updating coverage after renovations.** A kitchen remodel, addition, or finished basement can significantly increase your rebuild cost. Update your coverage after major improvements.

**Carrying the minimum liability limits.** $100,000 in liability sounds like a lot until you realize a serious injury lawsuit can easily exceed it. Umbrella policies providing $1M+ in additional liability coverage typically cost $200–$400/year.

**Ignoring the deductible/savings trade-off.** A higher deductible lowers your premium. If you can comfortably absorb a $2,500 or $5,000 loss, the premium savings over several years may exceed the deductible increase.

**Not doing a home inventory.** If you file a claim, you'll need to document what was lost or damaged. Without a home inventory, you'll underclaim. Use an app or video walkthrough to document your possessions.

## How to Find the Best Homeowners Insurance

### Step 1: Determine the Right Coverage Amount

- Calculate your home's rebuild cost (your insurer or a contractor can help estimate; square footage × local construction cost per square foot)
- Inventory your personal property to determine Coverage C needs
- Consider your liability exposure

### Step 2: Get Multiple Quotes

Homeowners insurance rates vary significantly between companies. Get at least 3–4 quotes:
- Direct insurers: State Farm, Allstate, Nationwide, Farmers
- Independent agents who access multiple carriers
- Online comparison tools: Policygenius, The Zebra, NerdWallet

### Step 3: Check Financial Strength Ratings

An insurance company that won't be around to pay your claim is worthless. Check AM Best, Moody's, or S&P ratings. Stick with insurers rated A- or better.

### Step 4: Review the Claims Process

Look up insurer reviews specifically around claims handling — not just pricing. JD Power publishes annual homeowners insurance satisfaction studies. An insurer with great prices but a reputation for fighting claims isn't a good deal.

### Step 5: Ask About Discounts

Common homeowners insurance discounts:
- Bundling with auto insurance (typically 5–15%)
- New home or recently renovated home
- Impact-resistant roofing
- Security system, smoke detectors, deadbolts
- Claims-free history
- Loyalty discounts
- Higher deductible

## How Homeowners Insurance Claims Work

When you file a claim:

1. **Contact your insurer promptly** — most policies require timely notice
2. **Document everything** — photos, video, receipts, repair estimates
3. **Prevent further damage** — you're responsible for reasonable mitigation (boarding up a broken window, drying out water)
4. **The adjuster's assessment** may not be final — you can negotiate or hire a public adjuster if you disagree with the estimate
5. **You pay your deductible** — the insurer covers the rest, up to your policy limits

**Think carefully before filing small claims.** Insurers can raise your rates or even non-renew your policy if you file too many claims, especially small ones. If the damage is close to your deductible, it may be cheaper to pay out of pocket and preserve your claims record.

## Bottom Line

Homeowners insurance is not optional for most people — lenders require it. But having a policy isn't the same as being adequately covered.

The most impactful things you can do right now:
1. Confirm your dwelling coverage equals your home's **rebuild cost**, not its market value
2. Check whether your personal property has replacement cost coverage
3. Evaluate whether you need flood, earthquake, or sewer backup riders based on your location
4. Schedule high-value items separately if they exceed your policy sublimits
5. Consider an umbrella policy for additional liability protection

A home inventory, completed today, will save you significant money and stress if you ever need to file a claim.
