Bankruptcy has a stigma that keeps people suffering under debt loads they have no realistic path out of. That stigma costs real people real years of their lives — grinding against interest charges that grow faster than they can pay, skipping medical care, losing sleep, watching garnishment eat their paychecks.

Here's the reality: bankruptcy is a federal legal remedy that exists because Congress decided, explicitly, that second chances have economic and social value. Millions of Americans use it successfully every year. In 2024, approximately 500,000 personal bankruptcy filings were processed in the U.S. Most of those people are in substantially better financial shape two to three years later.

This guide explains how personal bankruptcy actually works, who qualifies, what it costs, and whether it might be the right option for you.

*Note: Bankruptcy law is complex and varies by state. This is general information, not legal advice. Consult a bankruptcy attorney before making any decisions.*

## What Bankruptcy Is — and What It Isn't

Bankruptcy is a federal legal process that gives individuals and businesses relief from debt they cannot repay. The moment you file, an "automatic stay" kicks in. This immediately halts most collection actions — lawsuits, wage garnishments, foreclosure proceedings, and creditor phone calls.

**What bankruptcy can do:**
- Discharge (permanently eliminate) most unsecured debt
- Stop foreclosure temporarily — Chapter 13 can let you catch up on mortgage payments and keep your home
- Halt wage garnishments, pending lawsuits, and creditor contact
- Provide a structured repayment plan for debts that survive bankruptcy
- Give you a real financial fresh start

**What bankruptcy cannot do:**
- Discharge student loans in most cases (rare hardship exceptions exist, and the bar is genuinely high)
- Discharge taxes from the past 3 years (older tax debts can sometimes be discharged)
- Discharge alimony or child support
- Discharge debts arising from fraud or intentional wrongdoing
- Discharge most government fines and criminal restitution
- Make secured creditors' underlying security interests disappear (a mortgage lender can still pursue the home)

## Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13: The Core Difference

Most personal bankruptcies fall into one of two chapters. Choosing the right one matters enormously.

| Feature | Chapter 7 | Chapter 13 |
|---|---|---|
| How it works | Discharges most debt; no repayment | 3–5 year repayment plan |
| Timeline | 3–6 months | 3–5 years |
| Asset risk | Non-exempt assets can be liquidated | You keep all assets |
| Income requirement | Must pass means test | Available regardless of income |
| Good for | Unsecured debt you can't pay | Saving a home; non-exempt assets; high earners |
| Credit report | 10 years | 7 years |
| Filing fee | $338 | $313 |

The right choice depends on your income, assets, debt types, and goals. An attorney will help you identify which chapter fits your situation.

## Chapter 7: The Full Walkthrough

Chapter 7 is the "liquidation" bankruptcy. It's faster (3–6 months), simpler, and eliminates debt rather than restructuring it. But it comes with income limits and potential asset exposure.

### Who Qualifies: The Means Test

Chapter 7 requires passing a two-step means test.

**Step 1 — Income comparison:** Take your average monthly income over the past 6 months and compare it to the median income for your state and household size. If you're below the median, you automatically qualify.

State median income figures are updated regularly. As of early 2026, examples include:
- California: ~$78,000/year for a single person
- Texas: ~$60,000/year for a single person
- New York: ~$72,000/year for a single person

**Step 2 — Disposable income calculation:** If your income exceeds the median, you fill out a more detailed calculation using IRS-standard expense allowances. If your remaining disposable income after allowed expenses falls below a threshold, you still qualify.

Fail the means test? You can file Chapter 13 instead, or wait 6 months and refile if your income has dropped.

### What Happens to Your Property

The bankruptcy trustee can liquidate non-exempt assets to pay creditors. But every state has exemptions — property the law protects from creditors. Most people in Chapter 7 are "no-asset cases," meaning everything they own is covered by exemptions.

**Common exemptions (vary significantly by state):**

| Asset | Typical Protection Range |
|---|---|
| Home equity (homestead) | $25,000–Unlimited (Texas and Florida offer unlimited homestead protection) |
| Motor vehicle | $2,500–$10,000 equity |
| Retirement accounts (401k, IRA) | Generally fully protected |
| Household goods and clothing | $5,000–$15,000 combined |
| Tools of trade | $1,500–$10,000 |
| Life insurance cash value | Varies widely by state |

In practice: if you're a middle-class filer with modest home equity, a car worth less than your exemption, retirement savings, and the usual household goods — there's likely nothing for the trustee to take. Your case will be "no-asset."

If you have significant non-exempt assets you want to protect, Chapter 13 keeps everything.

### The Chapter 7 Process, Step by Step

**Step 1: Credit counseling (required)**
Federal law requires completing an approved credit counseling course within 180 days before filing. It's done online, takes 1–2 hours, and costs $10–$50. Nonprofit agencies like GreenPath and NFCC members offer it.

**Step 2: Prepare and file your petition**
You or your attorney prepare the bankruptcy petition — a thorough set of forms disclosing income, expenses, assets, debts, recent financial transactions, and financial history. This gets filed with the federal bankruptcy court in your district.

**Step 3: Automatic stay takes effect**
Immediately upon filing. Collection calls must stop. Wage garnishments halt. Foreclosure pauses.

**Step 4: Meeting of creditors (341 meeting)**
About 30 days after filing, you attend a meeting with the bankruptcy trustee. Creditors can attend but rarely do. The trustee asks questions under oath about your financial situation. Most meetings take 5–15 minutes. Your attorney is there with you.

**Step 5: Discharge**
If there are no complications, a discharge order enters roughly 60–90 days after the 341 meeting. The discharge legally eliminates your eligible debts — you no longer owe them and creditors cannot pursue you for them.

**Total timeline:** Most straightforward Chapter 7 cases close in 3–6 months.

### What Chapter 7 Costs

| Cost Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Court filing fee | $338 |
| Credit counseling course | ~$25 |
| Debtor education course (required after filing) | ~$25 |
| Attorney fees | $1,000–$2,500 |
| **Estimated total** | **$1,400–$2,900** |

Many bankruptcy attorneys offer payment plans, often structured so that fees are paid before filing. Filing without an attorney (pro se) is legally possible but significantly increases the risk of errors that cause dismissal or loss of assets.

## Chapter 13: The Full Walkthrough

Chapter 13 is the "reorganization" bankruptcy. You keep all your property and repay some or all of your debt over 3–5 years under a court-approved plan. It's more complex and takes longer, but it offers tools Chapter 7 doesn't.

### Who Should Consider Chapter 13

- Your income is too high to qualify for Chapter 7
- You're behind on mortgage payments and want to save your home — Chapter 13 is one of the few ways to do this
- You have significant non-exempt assets worth protecting
- You have tax debts or other priority debts that need structured repayment
- You've had a Chapter 7 discharge within the past 8 years (there's a mandatory waiting period between Chapter 7 filings; you can file Chapter 13 immediately)

### How the Repayment Plan Works

You propose a plan to the court covering 36 months (if your income is below median) or 60 months (if above median). The plan must pay:

1. **Secured creditors first:** Mortgage arrears, car loans you want to keep — brought current through the plan
2. **Priority debts second:** Recent taxes, back child support, alimony — paid in full
3. **Unsecured creditors last:** Credit cards, medical bills — paid whatever remains after #1 and #2, which can be a very small percentage of what you owe

**Concrete example:** You have $50,000 in credit card debt. Your plan has $400/month available for unsecured creditors over 60 months = $24,000 paid. The remaining $26,000 is discharged when you complete the plan. You paid 48 cents on the dollar on your credit card debt.

That's a simplified scenario — the actual calculation is more involved. But it illustrates why Chapter 13 can eliminate large amounts of debt while letting you keep property and catch up on secured debts.

### What Chapter 13 Can Do That Chapter 7 Can't

**Mortgage cure:** If you're 6 months behind on your mortgage, Chapter 13 lets you spread those arrears over the 5-year plan while resuming regular payments — and keep your home. This is the primary reason homeowners choose Chapter 13.

**Lien stripping:** If your home's value has fallen below what you owe on the first mortgage, a second mortgage may be entirely "underwater" — secured by nothing. Chapter 13 can strip that second mortgage lien and reclassify it as unsecured debt, potentially discharging it.

**Cram-down:** For cars and some investment property, Chapter 13 can sometimes reduce the debt to the current value of the collateral. If you owe $18,000 on a car worth $11,000, you may be able to pay only $11,000.

**Tax debts:** Priority tax debts get paid in full through the plan. Older tax debts that meet specific requirements can be treated as general unsecured debt.

### What Chapter 13 Costs

| Cost Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Court filing fee | $313 |
| Attorney fees | $3,000–$6,000+ |
| Credit and debtor education courses | ~$50 |
| **Estimated total** | **$3,400–$6,400+** |

Chapter 13 attorney fees are often paid partially through the repayment plan itself, which makes it more accessible upfront.

## How Bankruptcy Affects Your Credit

Chapter 7 stays on your credit report for 10 years from the filing date. Chapter 13 stays for 7 years.

The immediate impact is significant — scores typically fall 100–200 points. But for people who were already behind on payments, in collections, and dealing with judgments, the starting point is already damaged. The practical impact is often smaller than people expect.

**Recovery is faster than the stigma suggests:**

| Timeline After Discharge | What's Typically Possible |
|---|---|
| 6–12 months | Secured credit card; credit-builder loan |
| 12–24 months | Some unsecured credit cards; car financing (higher rates) |
| 2 years (Chapter 7) | FHA mortgage eligibility |
| 3 years | Conventional mortgage possible with strong post-bankruptcy history |
| 4–5 years | Many people are back to 700+ credit scores |

Lenders increasingly weigh recent payment history more heavily than a distant bankruptcy. Consistent, responsible credit use after discharge rebuilds scores faster than most people expect.

## Is Bankruptcy Right for Your Situation?

### Strong indicators bankruptcy may be appropriate:
- Minimum payments are consuming all discretionary income with no end in sight
- Wage garnishments are active or threatened
- Lawsuits from creditors are pending
- You're 3–6+ months behind on your mortgage and at risk of foreclosure
- Total unsecured debt exceeds 2–3 years of your net income
- You've tried debt negotiation and consolidation without success

### Situations where alternatives may be better:
- The financial crisis is temporary — you expect income to recover within 6 months
- Total debt is manageable with a realistic budget over 2–3 years
- Your debts are primarily non-dischargeable (student loans, recent taxes)
- You have significant non-exempt assets you'd lose in Chapter 7

### Alternatives worth understanding first:

**Debt settlement:** Creditors sometimes settle unsecured debt for 40–60 cents on the dollar for a lump sum. This damages credit but avoids bankruptcy. Warning: for-profit debt settlement companies often charge 15–25% of the enrolled debt in fees and take 2–4 years to negotiate — often leaving clients in worse shape. Nonprofit credit counseling agencies offering debt management plans are generally more trustworthy.

**Negotiating directly:** If you contact your creditors directly — honestly, with a realistic proposal — some will work with you on reduced interest rates or temporary payment reductions. This works best early, before accounts go to collections.

## Finding a Bankruptcy Attorney

Bankruptcy forms are complex. Filing errors can result in dismissal, loss of exemptions you were entitled to, or having your discharge denied. The difference between a properly exempted case and an improperly exempted one can be tens of thousands of dollars.

**Where to find a bankruptcy attorney:**
- National Association of Consumer Bankruptcy Attorneys (NACBA) directory at nacba.org — find attorneys who specialize in consumer bankruptcy, not general practice
- Your state bar association's referral service
- Legal aid organizations for income-qualifying individuals — often handle bankruptcies free or reduced cost

Initial consultations are typically free. A good bankruptcy attorney will tell you honestly whether bankruptcy makes sense, which chapter fits your situation, and what to expect. If an attorney is pushing you toward bankruptcy without thoroughly assessing your situation, find another one.

## Common Questions

**Will my employer find out I filed bankruptcy?**
Not automatically. Chapter 7 is a public filing, but employers don't routinely monitor bankruptcy filings. Chapter 13 plans sometimes involve payroll deductions, which your employer processes — but cannot legally act on. Federal law prohibits government employers from terminating employees solely for filing bankruptcy.

**Can I keep my house?**
In Chapter 7: if your home equity falls within your state's homestead exemption, yes. If equity exceeds the exemption, you'd need to pay the difference to keep the home, or file Chapter 13. In Chapter 13: yes, as long as you resume current payments and pay arrears through the plan.

**Can I keep my car?**
If you're current on payments and your equity is within the exemption, usually yes — you'll either "reaffirm" the debt or continue payments. If you're behind, Chapter 13 lets you catch up.

**What about cosigners?**
Your discharge protects you, not your cosigners. If a friend or family member cosigned your debt, they remain fully liable after your discharge. Chapter 13's "co-debtor stay" provides some temporary protection, but it's limited.

**Can I file again if I've filed before?**
Yes, with waiting periods. Between Chapter 7 filings: 8 years. Chapter 7 after Chapter 13: 4 years. Chapter 13 after Chapter 7: 2 years. Chapter 13 after Chapter 13: 2 years.

## Bottom Line

Bankruptcy is a legal tool. It exists because society recognizes that people fall into unmanageable debt through medical crises, job loss, divorce, and poor decisions — and that permanent destitution serves no one's interests.

If you're genuinely drowning in debt with no realistic path out — not a bad month, but a genuinely untenable situation — a consultation with a bankruptcy attorney costs nothing and takes an hour. Most will tell you honestly whether bankruptcy is the right tool or whether alternatives make more sense.

The stigma is not worth years of grinding poverty under debt you'll never actually pay off. If the legal remedy is there and you qualify, using it is rational.
